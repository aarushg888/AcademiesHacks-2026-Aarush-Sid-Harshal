Warren Buffett on moats: "A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital. The dynamics of capitalism guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business 'castle' that is earning high returns."

Warren Buffett on price vs value: "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get. Whether we're talking about socks or stocks, I like buying quality merchandise when it is marked down."

Warren Buffett on patience: "The stock market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient. Our favorite holding period is forever."

Warren Buffett on understanding the business: "Never invest in a business you cannot understand. I don't look to jump over 7-foot bars; I look around for 1-foot bars that I can step over."

Warren Buffett on management: "We look for three things when we hire people: intelligence, initiative, and integrity. And if they don't have the third, the first two will kill you. When we find a business with excellent economics run by honest and able management, our goal is to own it forever."

Warren Buffett on margin of safety: "The three most important words in investing are 'margin of safety.' We never want to depend on the kindness of strangers. Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1."

Warren Buffett on debt: "We will reject interesting opportunities rather than over-leverage our balance sheet. We use debt sparingly. When we do borrow, we attempt to structure our loans on a long-term fixed-rate basis."

Warren Buffett on circle of competence: "What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word 'selected': You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence."

Warren Buffett on fear and greed: "We simply attempt to be fearful when others are greedy and to be greedy only when others are fearful. The best thing that happens to us is when a great company gets into temporary trouble."

Warren Buffett on retained earnings: "Unrestricted earnings should be retained only when there is a reasonable prospect that for every dollar retained by the corporation, at least one dollar of market value will be created for owners."

Warren Buffett on diversification: "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you are doing. Wide diversification is only required when investors do not understand what they are doing."

Warren Buffett on consumer brands: "The products or services that have wide, sustainable moats around them are the ones that deliver rewards to investors. Businesses with durable competitive advantages — Coca-Cola, See's Candies, American Express — earn extraordinary returns on tangible assets."

Warren Buffett on capital allocation: "The job of a CEO is capital allocation. It's the most important job. After 10 years on the job, a CEO whose company retains earnings equal to 10% of net worth will have been responsible for the deployment of more than 60% of all the capital at work in the business."

Warren Buffett on market timing: "I have no idea what the stock market is going to do next month or next year. But I'm confident in where it will be in 10 or 20 years — much higher. We've never had a period where I could say the future looks perfectly clear."

Warren Buffett on consistent earnings: "We favor businesses that have demonstrated consistent earning power over those that are projecting 'turnaround' situations or relying on future growth. We want predictability. Look for companies that have shown they can perform in good times and bad."

Warren Buffett on owner earnings: "What really counts is owner earnings — net income plus depreciation minus capex. That is the cash a business can distribute to shareholders while maintaining its competitive position."

Warren Buffett on inflation: "The best businesses during inflation are those that require minimal capital reinvestment to maintain their economic position — brands, toll-road businesses, media franchises. Businesses that are capital-intensive suffer most in inflation."

Warren Buffett on mistakes of omission: "Our biggest mistakes have been mistakes of omission, not commission. Sucking my thumb while Walmart grew, while Google went public at what I thought was too high a price — those are the ones that cost us real money."
