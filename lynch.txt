Peter Lynch on investing in what you know: "Invest in what you know. The amateur investor has numerous built-in advantages that, if exploited, will result in outperforming experts. Your local knowledge is an edge — you see product trends before Wall Street does."

Peter Lynch on the PEG ratio: "The P/E ratio tells you how much you're paying. The growth rate tells you what you're getting. The PEG ratio — P/E divided by growth rate — is the most useful number in my toolkit. A PEG below 1.0 is where I look first."

Peter Lynch on ten-baggers: "Searching for the ten-bagger — a stock that goes up tenfold — requires owning stocks of companies that are still growing rapidly but are not yet universally recognized. The best opportunities are in companies too small for Wall Street to cover."

Peter Lynch on categorizing stocks: "I divide stocks into six categories: slow growers, stalwarts, fast growers, cyclicals, asset plays, and turnarounds. Each requires a different holding period and a different set of expectations."

Peter Lynch on avoiding complexity: "The simpler the business, the better I like it. If you can't explain a business in three sentences, it's probably too complicated to understand well enough to own. I'm suspicious of any business that needs a PhD to explain its product."

Peter Lynch on the two-minute drill: "Before buying any stock, I do a two-minute monologue about why I'm buying it. If I can't say it out loud in simple terms — what the company does, why it will grow, what could go wrong — I don't buy it."

Peter Lynch on institutional blindspots: "Institutional investors are forced to own large-cap stocks. Small-cap companies that are too small for institutions are where individual investors can find real opportunities. Under-followed equals undervalued more often than not."

Peter Lynch on retail observations: "Pay attention to what you see as a consumer. If you love a restaurant and all your friends love it but it only has 20 locations, that could be the early stages of a growth story. I bought Dunkin Donuts because I loved the coffee."

Peter Lynch on earnings growth: "Earnings growth is the engine that drives stock prices over the long term. A company growing earnings at 20% per year will eventually see its stock price reflect that. Everything else is noise."

Peter Lynch on debt in growth companies: "A fast-growing company with a strong balance sheet and no debt is a gift. A fast-growing company drowning in debt is a trap. Check the debt-to-equity ratio before you celebrate the growth rate."

Peter Lynch on turnarounds: "Turnarounds seldom turn. When I do invest in a turnaround, I want to see: management change, a clearly identified reason for prior failure, a simple fix already in progress, and a healthy balance sheet to survive the recovery period."

Peter Lynch on selling: "Selling your winners and holding your losers is like pulling out the flowers and watering the weeds. I hold winners as long as the story is intact and the valuation remains reasonable."

Peter Lynch on macro ignorance: "I don't know what interest rates are going to do. I don't know what the economy is going to do. Nobody does. What I do know is whether this company's products are selling, whether its earnings are growing, and whether the stock is reasonably priced."

Peter Lynch on dividends: "Dividends act as a constant reminder that a company's earnings are real. Companies that consistently pay and raise dividends tend to be better run than those that don't — management is accountable to the cash commitment."

Peter Lynch on the importance of homework: "People spend more time researching a car to buy than a stock to own. This is backwards. A stock is a piece of a business. Treat it that way and do real research — visit the store, use the product, read the annual report."
