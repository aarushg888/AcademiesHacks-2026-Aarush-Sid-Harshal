Charlie Munger on mental models: "You must know the big ideas in the big disciplines and use them routinely. I'm a generalist. I use multiple mental models from multiple disciplines — physics, biology, psychology, economics, mathematics — and I think that makes me a much better investor than a pure finance person."

Charlie Munger on inversion: "Invert, always invert. Tell me where I'm going to die so I won't go there. It's not enough to think about what can go right — spend equal time thinking about what can go wrong and work backwards from failure."

Charlie Munger on incentives: "Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives. Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome. Incentives are the most powerful force in business."

Charlie Munger on the lollapalooza effect: "When multiple forces work together in the same direction you get lollapalooza effects — results that are hugely powerful in one direction. The best businesses have multiple competitive advantages that reinforce each other."

Charlie Munger on patience and opportunity: "The big money is not in the buying and the selling but in the waiting. We have three baskets: in, out, and too tough. If something is too tough, we move on. Our competitive advantage is patience combined with rationality."

Charlie Munger on learning: "Go to bed smarter than when you woke up. I have known no wise person over a broad subject matter area who didn't read all the time — none, zero. You'd be amazed at how much Warren reads and at how much I read."

Charlie Munger on quality over price: "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price. I pushed Warren away from pure Grahamite thinking toward paying for quality."

Charlie Munger on moats through psychology: "Many competitive advantages come from psychology. People underestimate the power of brand, habit, and social proof. Coca-Cola sells happiness. That's not rational — it's psychological — and that's exactly why it endures."

Charlie Munger on businesses to avoid: "There are businesses that are so bad that even a great manager can't save them. I've seen plenty of people with high IQs destroy value because they were in terrible businesses with no moat and bad economics."

Charlie Munger on concentration: "The idea of excessive diversification is madness. Wide diversification, which necessarily includes investment in mediocre businesses, makes winning outcomes impossible. A few great businesses held forever beats a portfolio of adequate ones."

Charlie Munger on management character: "We look for three things: integrity, intelligence, and energy. And if they don't have the first, the other two will kill you. We also look for managers who treat their employees and customers well — it's correlated with long-term success."

Charlie Munger on avoiding envy and debt: "Envy is the stupidest of the seven deadly sins because you can't have any fun with it. And debt is its twin — people borrow to consume, not to produce. A business drowning in debt cannot compound."

Charlie Munger on checklist thinking: "No wise pilot, no matter how great his natural talent and experience, fails to use a checklist when doing a preflight. We use checklists too — not rules, but thinking frameworks that force us to consider the things most likely to go wrong."

Charlie Munger on skin in the game: "I want management that thinks like owners because they are owners. Huge stock option packages that don't align with shareholder timelines are a red flag. I want the CEO to lose money when I lose money."

Charlie Munger on accounting red flags: "The number one rule of accounting: don't trust accounting. Earnings can be manipulated. Cash flow is harder to fake. I look for businesses where reported earnings are close to cash earnings and where management is conservative in its accounting assumptions."
